Medusa Across Literary and Artistic Sources

Research question: How is Medusa represented across literary and artistic sources?

Medusa: Myth and Representation

In Greek mythology, Medusa was one of the three Gorgons. Her sisters were Stheno and Euryale, and the three were commonly described as children of Phorcys and Ceto. Unlike her immortal sisters, Medusa was mortal.

Medusa is generally represented with living snakes in place of hair. Her gaze causes petrification. The hero Perseus killed her while looking at her reflection in a polished shield. After the beheading of Medusa, Pegasus and Chrysaor emerged. Perseus later gave the head to Athena, who placed it on her aegis.

Ancient traditions located the Gorgons in distant lands, including Libya. In classical antiquity, the image of the Gorgon's head became the Gorgoneion, an image associated with apotropaic protection.

A late and influential account appears in Ovid's Metamorphoses. In this version, Neptune is associated with Medusa in the temple of Minerva, after which the goddess changes Medusa's admired hair into snakes. This transformation shifts Medusa's representation from beautiful maiden to terrifying figure.

Artistic representations emphasize different moments and meanings. Caravaggio's Medusa, held by the Uffizi Gallery in Florence, presents the severed head on a shield. Peter Paul Rubens's Head of Medusa, with animals attributed to Frans Snyders, depicts the aftermath with snakes and blood; it is held by the Kunsthistorisches Museum in Vienna. Benvenuto Cellini's Perseus with the Head of Medusa presents the victorious hero and is installed at the Loggia dei Lanzi.

Ovidian Literary Evidence

In Book IV, Perseus narrates how he viewed Medusa in a reflected bronze shield and struck her head from her neck. The passage connects her death with the birth of Pegasus and Chrysaor.

"the swift winged horse Pegasus and his brother the warrior Chrysaor, were born from their mother's blood."Ovid, Metamorphoses, Book IV

Ovid then explains the snakes through a retrospective account of Medusa's beauty and Minerva's response, establishing a literary transformation that strongly shaped later visual culture.

"she changed the Gorgon's hair to foul snakes."Ovid, Metamorphoses, Book IV
